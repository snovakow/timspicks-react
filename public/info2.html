Short answer: neither simple mean-scaling nor mean-shifting is ideal. The correct way to normalize sportsbook anytime goal probabilities is to remove the bookmaker margin (vig) and then normalize multiplicatively across all players — not by aligning means.

Here’s why, and what actually works:

🔴 Why mean-scaling or mean-shifting is flawed

1. Mean scaling (multiplying to match averages)

  • This assumes the distribution shape is already correct, just mis-scaled.

  • But sportsbook odds include non-uniform vig — favorites are taxed differently than longshots.

  • Result: you distort relative probabilities (e.g., overrate mid-tier players).

2. Mean shifting (adding/subtracting)

  • This is worse mathematically:

    • Can produce negative probabilities or >1

    • Breaks proportional relationships between players

  • Probabilities are inherently multiplicative, not additive.

✅ Correct approach: Vig removal + normalization

Step 1: Convert odds → implied probability

For decimal odds:

p_i = 1 / odds_i

Step 2: Remove vig (normalize within a game or team)

For a given set of players (important choice, more below):

p_i_fair = p_i / sum(p_all_players)

This ensures:

  • Total probability mass is consistent

  • You remove the bookmaker overround

Step 3 (optional but powerful): Cross-book aggregation

If you're scraping multiple books:

  • Convert each book → fair probabilities (above)

  • Then aggregate using:

    • Mean of probabilities (simple, effective)

    • OR log-odds averaging (more robust for extremes)

Log-odds method:

logit(p) = log(p / (1 - p))
avg_logit = mean(logit(p_i across books))
p_final = sigmoid(avg_logit)

The key insight (this is the edge you're looking for):

You should normalize across only relevant scoring sets, not all players blindly.

Best options:

  • Normalize within a single game (both teams combined) ✔️ best

  • Or normalize per team, then adjust by team goal expectation

Why?

Because:

  • Anytime goal probabilities are not independent

  • Total scoring is constrained (game total, team totals)

  • 3 players each having 60% anytime probability (impossible jointly)

Correct — and this is the core misunderstanding most people hit when working with anytime goal scorer markets.

👉 Anytime goal probabilities are not supposed to sum to 1.

🧠 Why they don’t add to 1

Because each “anytime goal” bet is:

“Player i scores at least one goal”

These events are:

  • ✅ Not mutually exclusive (multiple players can score)

  • ❌ Not collectively exhaustive (a game can have 0 goals, or many goals)

So mathematically:

∑ P ( player i scores )    >    1 \sum P(\text{player i scores}) \;>\; 1 ∑P(player i scores)>1

In fact, the sum roughly tracks:

Expected total number of goal scorers in the game

🔢 Intuition

If a game is expected to have ~6 goals:

  • You might see ~10–14 players each with 20–60% anytime probability

  • The sum might be 4–7+, not 1

That’s correct behavior, not an error.

⚠️ Why normalization to 1 is wrong

If you force:

∑ p i = 1 \sum p_i = 1 ∑pi​=1

you are implicitly saying:

“Exactly one of these players will score”

That turns the problem into a mutually exclusive outcome model — which is completely different from reality.

This will:

  • Underestimate top players

  • Overinflate longshots

  • Destroy calibration for multi-pick strategies (like Tim Hortons)

✅ What you should normalize to

Instead of forcing sum = 1, you want:

Option A: Normalize to expected goals (better)

If total expected goals in game = G G G:

∑ p i ≈ G \sum p_i \approx G ∑pi​≈G

This is much more realistic.

Option B: Work in independent probabilities (simplest)

Just leave them as-is (after vig removal):

  • Treat each player as a Bernoulli:

    P i = anytime goal probability P_i = \text{anytime goal probability} Pi​=anytime goal probability

  • Then for 3 picks:

P ( all 3 hit ) = P 1 ⋅ P 2 ⋅ P 3 ⋅ correlation adjustment P(\text{all 3 hit}) = P_1 \cdot P_2 \cdot P_3 \cdot \text{correlation adjustment} P(all 3 hit)=P1​⋅P2​⋅P3​⋅correlation adjustment

🔥 Key insight for your use case (this matters)

For something like the Tim Hortons challenge:

You should NOT normalize across players at all

Instead:

  1. Convert odds → fair probabilities (remove vig)

  2. Keep them independent

  3. Select players to maximize:

P 1 ⋅ P 2 ⋅ P 3 P_1 \cdot P_2 \cdot P_3 P1​⋅P2​⋅P3​

  • Same team → positive correlation
